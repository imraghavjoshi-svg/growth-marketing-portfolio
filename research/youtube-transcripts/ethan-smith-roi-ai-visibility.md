## Transcript

Today's topic is AI visibility optimization. We have Ethan Smith, CEO and founder of Graphite. Graphite.io works with companies like Webflow, Notion, Ripling, Column and they try to do experiments and analysis and then feed that into what they recommend.

On LLM growth:
LLMs are growing 100% year-over-year across all LLMs. Interestingly, Google is flat — down 0.009% year-over-year, essentially flat. LLMs are not taking market share from Google — it's incremental. The pie is getting bigger. If you do search, the slice of the pie for search is staying the same, it's just that the pie is getting bigger.

8 to 10% of signups are coming from LLMs for multiple B2B engineering-forward companies. So it's not a potential hypothetical channel that might be important in the future — it's currently impactful. The traffic is also really well-qualified — the conversion rate tends to be higher.

How AI generates responses:
When you ask an LLM a question, it does a search and then summarizes the search results. There's the core model and there's the RAG piece. The core model is next word prediction, reasoning and planning. The RAG piece would be summarizing a list of things. The decision matrix probably came from the core model, but the list of recommendations is a summarization of search results.

The more times you get mentioned in the citations, the better. Many questions start with a search and their search engine works very similar to Bing, works very similar to Google. So SEO — much of SEO applies for AI optimization.

On topics and keyword clustering:
In SEO a topic is a cluster of many keywords and the same is true for answer engine optimization. One page can answer all of these questions. When you ask a question in Perplexity, it gets transformed into keywords — these keywords are essentially the canonical keywords. If you have many different keywords, there's a canonical center point of that group of keywords and a question gets mapped to the canonical center of the topic group and then gets transformed into a search version.

On how to get mentions:
There's on-site and off-site. On-site is your page on your website URL. Off-site is any other website. The more general the question, the more you have to be mentioned by others to win. The more specific it is, the more likely your own pages will work.

For the head terms — the most popular questions — you have to get mentioned off-site. The tail is larger and longer in LLMs than in SEO. How to rank for the long tail is the exact same as SEO, but it's bigger. You need to answer questions that many of which have never been searched for before.

Off-site sources that feed AI citations for B2B SaaS: listicles, YouTube, Reddit, affiliate sites, Zapier, G2. You would have a strategy for each individual one.

On Reddit strategy:
General suggestion with Reddit is to be a real person and to post useful comments and to not sell your stuff. The community is quite robust in their community management. Find which threads to comment on by putting all your prompts that you want to win and then getting top-cited domains and top-cited URLs. For domain equals Reddit, those threads are the ones you care about. You can also do alerts — when there's a thread about it containing keyword, post in your Slack.

On YouTube strategy:
The less entertaining your product, the better it is to do video. The most entertaining things are the most crowded — travel, beauty, food. The least entertaining things are the least crowded — B2B SaaS, AI API for payment processing. That niche non-entertaining stuff is perfect for video because there's just not that many videos. With video you don't have a community saying they don't like your comment. You don't need that many views in a lot of cases to have your video appear, and you can control this — you cannot control Reddit, you can control making videos.

On competitor pages strategy:
A key strategy is to make a list of all your competitors on your own website. So if you are Ripling, you make a page saying here are these other companies — ADP, Gusto, etc. Then you appear when someone asks what's the best payroll management software. ADP appears because they have a feature page and a page which is a list of all their competitors.

Another strategy: if you want to integrate with all the big HR systems, make an integration page with Ripling and Gusto and ADP. Then ping them and say you did a page about your integration with them — would they please do one about you? And then they will do an integration page about you and now the big incumbents are linking to you.

On consensus building:
When the LLM is asked what's the best tool for X, it's trying to look for multiple citations saying a particular answer. The more of the citations that say you, the more likely you are to be the answer. So if you have all these sites sort of combining together and all saying that you're the best, then you become the best. The LLM says the consensus is surfer, clearscope, this company — not SCM rush. Now you're the best.

On content format for AI:
Don't need special markdown variations. ChatGPT OpenAI has some of the most talented engineers in the world — they're going to be able to parse HTML and CSS and JavaScript. What to do: lists, bullet points, headers, questions, and schema. The key new thing is marking up your forms and buttons so that AI agents can do conversions on your website without sending a user there.

On whether to create new content or rewrite existing:
You don't need to redo your existing SEO content for LLMs. Maybe add some tables and bullet points and some structure. But you don't need a brand new version of your page.

On tracking AI visibility:
Track the beginning — did I appear for the prompts I care about? And the end — do they tell me they converted from that? Ask a how did you hear about us question. The in-between is very hard to track because there's not that much clicking happening. It's usually people opening a new tab or searching Google for the thing they just saw in the answer, which you misattribute to direct or branded search.

Key insight from case study:
Secured a placement on a really small agency website with only 6,000 monthly traffic from Google. Discovered in AI tracker it was a really frequently cited source. After securing the spot, visibility jumped from on-and-off to getting cited almost every time. You can find legit sources that will help you a lot in LLMs that are not that appreciated by Google.

On what determines who gets shown in AI engines:
It's on-site and off-site. On-site would be whoever does SEO the best. Off-site would be getting mentioned as much as possible depending on your category. The combination of those two things determines AI visibility.

On strengthening AEO while maintaining Google SEO performance:
Everything for Google works for free for LLMs. The new things to do beyond SEO are off-site optimization and filling in the long tail. They are not different strategies — they go hand in hand.
